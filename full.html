Module 01

The Sailor's Creed

Identity & Ethos

Your identity as a United States Sailor begins with the Sailor's Creed.

“I am a United States Sailor. I will support and defend the Constitution of the United States of America and I will obey the orders of those appointed over me.”

“I proudly serve my country's Navy combat team with Honor, Courage and Commitment. I am committed to excellence and the fair treatment of all.”

In this course, treat the Sailor's Creed as your anchor. Read it aloud, reflect on each line, and connect it to your personal reasons for joining. The Creed ties directly into the Navy Core Values you will study next.

Module 02

Navy Core Values

Honor
Courage
Commitment

For 250 years, Sailors have stood tall for the principles that make America strong.

“In the Navy, these unchanging principles – Honor, Courage and Commitment – are known as Core Values. To know, understand and faithfully live by them is the duty of all members of the U.S. Navy.”

This module breaks down each Core Value into practical expectations for your daily conduct as a Future Sailor and future member of the fleet.

Honor

Honor is about integrity in every relationship and decision. The guide explains that Sailors must “conduct ourselves in the highest ethical manner in all relationships with peers, superiors and subordinates” and “abide by an uncompromising code of integrity, taking responsibility for our actions and keeping our word.”

  • Ethical conduct: Be honest and truthful in all dealings, inside and outside the Navy.
  • Responsibility: Own your actions and follow through on commitments.
  • 24/7 standard: Live up to legal and ethical responsibilities at all times, not just on duty.

Courage

Courage is the moral and mental strength to do what is right, even when it is difficult. The guide notes that Sailors must “make decisions in the best interest of the Navy and the nation, without regard to personal consequences” and meet challenges “while adhering to a higher standard of personal conduct and decency.”

  • Operational courage: Face hazardous or demanding missions with professionalism.
  • Moral courage: Speak up and act correctly even when it is unpopular.
  • Stewardship: Use resources entrusted to you honestly and efficiently.

Commitment

Commitment is about caring for people and relentlessly improving. The guide emphasizes that Sailors will “demand respect up and down the chain of command” and “be committed to positive change and constant improvement.”

  • Respect: Treat everyone with dignity, regardless of race, religion or sex.
  • Team focus: Work together to improve the quality of your work, your people and yourself.
  • Excellence: Strive for technical mastery and high moral character in your rating and duties.

Module 03

Warrior Toughness & Mindset Skills

Mind
Body
Spirit

Warrior Toughness is the Navy’s deliberate approach to optimizing human performance.

“The ability to take a hit and keep going is critical in warfare and in life. Boot camp focuses on developing the tools you will need to perform at increasingly demanding levels of performance for the situation at hand.”

Warrior Toughness starts with your “Why”—your personal Warrior Ethos—and then builds skills across mind, body and spirit so you can perform under pressure and recover quickly from stress.

Key Warrior Toughness Mindset Skills

Warrior Ethos

A written statement of your personal values and purpose. It connects you to a greater mission and helps you act with character and decisiveness. Review and refine it regularly.

Healthy Behaviors

Balanced strengthening of mind, body and spirit. Treat yourself as a high-performing asset—sleep, nutrition, movement and spiritual practices all matter.

Performance Statements

Brief, realistic phrases you repeat to stay focused and motivated during stress. Develop them before challenging evolutions and use them in the moment.

Recalibrate (Breathing)

A breathing technique to reduce the negative effects of stress or adrenaline. Practice routinely so you can use it without needing a time-out.

Mindfulness

Present-moment awareness of yourself and your situation. It helps you respond deliberately instead of reacting emotionally in challenging environments.

Goal Setting

A roadmap of personal milestones for continuous improvement. Establish, implement and review goals while tracking progress.

Mental Rehearsal

Vividly imagine successful execution of a task. This builds confidence and prepares you for high-risk, high-stakes operations.

Peer-to-Peer Support

You are responsible for observing and supporting your shipmates. Proactive communication builds connection, inclusion and performance.

Energy Management

Match your energy to the demands of the situation. Learn to recognize your own energy level and adjust as needed.

Intentional Recovery

Deliberately reset mind, body and spirit between challenges. This maintains performance over time and builds resilience.

Flexible Thinking

Think about situations in new or different ways. Anticipate, plan and adapt to evolving conditions; embrace uncertainty.

Sea Stories

Real experiences that highlight skills and values. Sharing and reflecting on sea stories reinforces the consequences of choices and the importance of Navy culture.

Warrior Mindset Cycle

The guide describes a four-phase Warrior Mindset—Commitment, Preparation, Execution and Reflection—that separates good commands from great ones.

  • Commitment: Define your purpose and philosophy for living and serving.
  • Preparation: Train mind, body and spirit rigorously; “when you're under pressure, you don't rise to the occasion—you sink to your level of training.”
  • Execution: Apply Warrior Toughness skills under real pressure, minimizing the “Fog of War.”
  • Reflection: Capture lessons learned and continuously improve as an individual and as a team.

Module 04

Define Your Warrior Ethos

Your “Why”

Your Warrior Ethos is your guiding compass—who you are, what you fight for, and where you’re going.

“Your Warrior Ethos forms the foundation for developing your fighting spirit throughout boot camp. Who you are, what your values are, what you are willing to fight for, all inform your goals and help drive you as a warrior in the United States Navy.”

Guided Reflection (from the START Guide)

Use these prompts (adapted directly from the guide) to write your own Warrior Ethos statement:

  1. What has made you who you are? Think about family, mentors, teams, organizations or life events.
  2. What are you willing to fight for? For yourself (self-respect, success), for the greater good (family, vulnerable people), or for a higher power (God or anything transcendent).
  3. What values show up in your “why”? Examples: truth, hope, love, honor, freedom, honesty, respect.
  4. What are your goals for what you’re willing to fight for? List concrete outcomes you want to see.
  5. Write your Warrior Ethos statement. Summarize who you are (identity), what you are fighting for (connections), what you believe (values) and where you are going (destination).

Module 05

Parents, Spouse & RTC Graduation

Family Readiness

Future Sailors don’t walk this path alone—family support is part of the Navy story.

Welcome Aboard, Parents and Spouses

“Welcome to the Navy family. We know you are very proud of your family member and we would like to share with you the many superb career and educational opportunities that are available in the Navy.”

The guide encourages families to attend local Delayed Entry Program (DEP) meetings. These meetings are mandatory for Future Sailors and help everyone understand what to expect from Recruit Training Command (RTC).

  • Stay informed: Attend DEP meetings when invited.
  • Ask questions: Recruiters are a resource for both Future Sailors and families.
  • Support through letters: Mail during boot camp can be a major morale boost.

RTC Graduation

“Recruit graduation means public recognition for all the hard work, determination and perseverance required to complete Recruit Training. Families are a big part of the reason for your Recruit's success and you have every right to feel proud.”

Access to the base on graduation day is restricted to those on the access list. Each graduating Sailor is responsible for identifying who will attend, so coordinate early with your Future Sailor.

  • Check official info: Visit bootcamp.navy.mil before making travel plans.
  • Access list: Only those listed by the Sailor will be allowed on base for graduation.
  • Emotional support: Letters and encouragement help Recruits handle the “initial shock” of military life.

When Recruiters Transfer

The guide explains that transitions are normal in the Navy. If a Recruiter transfers, another Recruiter “relieves the watch” and assumes responsibility for preparing the Future Sailor for boot camp.

Module 06

Accommodation of Religious Practices

Policy Awareness

The Navy seeks to accommodate religious practices when it does not impact readiness or discipline.

“Department of the Navy policy is to accommodate the doctrinal or traditional observances of the religious faith practiced by individual members when they will not have an adverse impact on military readiness, individual or unit readiness, unit cohesion, health, safety or discipline.”

Accommodation is not guaranteed in every situation and depends on military necessity. The Commanding Officer has the authority to determine when mission requirements limit accommodation.

Key Points for Future Sailors

  • In-person explanation: Recruiting personnel must meet with you to explain the policy.
  • Written request: If you desire an accommodation, you must inform your Recruiter and submit the request in writing.
  • Written approval: A designated authority must approve the accommodation in writing before you are eligible to enlist.

Module 07

Delayed Entry Program & Standards

Standards
Integrity

Your time in the Delayed Entry Program (DEP) is preparation time—physically, mentally and administratively.

Body Composition & Physical Readiness

The guide states that “Recruits must fall within approved body composition measurements. Make the most of all physical training opportunities during the Delayed Entry Program.” Use this time to build healthy habits and meet Navy standards.

Tattoo & Body Art Markings

Once you enlist into the DEP, obtaining additional tattoos is prohibited until you complete basic training. New tattoos or piercings can risk infection or disqualify you.

  • Content restrictions: Tattoos that are obscene, sexually explicit, discriminatory, or affiliated with gangs, supremacist or extremist groups, or illegal drug use are prohibited.
  • Location restrictions: No tattoos on the head, face (including ears) or scalp (including hairline).
  • Authorized areas: One tattoo on the back of the neck (max one inch in any dimension) and visible tattoos below the elbow or knee are allowed, as long as content is acceptable.
  • Uniform visibility: Torso tattoos must not be visible through white uniform clothing.

Your Responsibility to Your Recruiter

  • Civility: Treat Recruiters with the same courtesy and respect they provide you.
  • Honesty: Be truthful when providing information for your enlistment application.
  • Updates: Notify your Recruiter of any changes in education, health, police involvement, drug use or dependency.
  • Required viewing: Ensure you have viewed “The Faces of Boot Camp” and “Sexual Assault Prevention & Response (SAPR-D)” videos.

Recruiter Responsibilities (What They Must Not Do)

  • Coach or entice you to provide false statements, records or documents.
  • Bribe or coerce you to process for enlistment.
  • Intervene with police or judicial authorities on your behalf.
  • Form dating or private, unofficial social relationships with you.
  • Engage in unofficial financial or business dealings with you.
  • Transport you in their personal vehicle, gamble with you, or solicit/accept anything of value from you.

Warning Order & UCMJ Article 83

“The information you provide and/or acknowledge receipt of is considered a statement of fact to the best of your knowledge. Should you provide or acknowledge information that is knowingly false, you may be prosecuted in accordance with the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ).”

Providing false information about police involvement, prior service, education, dependents, drug use, medical history, age or other enlistment factors can lead to charges under UCMJ Article 83 (Fraudulent Enlistment, Appointment, or Separation).

Module 08

Advancement Opportunities in DEP

Paygrade Acceleration

The Referral Recognition Program and prior achievements can advance you to E-2 or E-3 before or at RTC.

“One of the benefits of Delayed Entry Program is the Referral Recognition Program… If anyone you refer enlists in the Navy, you may be advanced to pay grade E-2 or E-3.”

Advancement to E-2

  • Referrals: One enlisted contract (Nuclear Field or Special Operations), or two enlisted contracts (non-Nuclear Field/Special Operations), or one officer accession.
  • JROTC: Completion of two years in JROTC.
  • Education: 24 semester hours or 36 quarter credit hours at an accredited institution, or 1,080 classroom hours at an accredited vocational institute.
  • Military academies: Two years and graduation from an accredited high school-level military academy.
  • Youth programs: Advancement to E-2 in Naval Sea Cadet program, Quartermaster in Sea Scouts, E-5 in Young Marines, or Civil Air Patrol Billy Mitchell Award.
  • At RTC: Passing a Recruit Division Commander's assessment at boot camp.

Advancement to E-3

  • Referrals: Two enlisted contracts (Nuclear Field or Special Operations), four enlisted contracts (non-Nuclear Field/Special Operations), or two officer accessions.
  • JROTC: Completion of three years in JROTC.
  • Education: 48+ semester hours or 72+ quarter credit hours at an accredited institution, or 2,160 classroom hours at an accredited vocational institute.
  • Youth programs: Advancement to E-3 in Naval Sea Cadet program, or completion of Eagle Scout or Girl Scout Gold Award.

Note: All requirements must be met prior to reporting to RTC. Keep documentation organized and ready to present.

Module 09

RTC Preparations & Navy Knowledge

Boot Camp Ready

You are required to know and recite key Navy knowledge before completing Recruit Training.

Mission of the Navy

“The mission of the Navy is to maintain, train and equip combat-ready naval forces capable of winning wars, deterring aggression and maintaining freedom of the seas.”

Vision of the Navy

“We are an integrated naval force that will provide maritime dominance for the nation… Our Core Values and accountability at the individual and organizational levels will shape our culture and guide our actions.”

The Eleven General Orders of a Sentry

You must be able to quote any one—or all—of the Eleven General Orders of a Sentry from memory by the fifth day of the first week of training. The guide states that “it is imperative that you learn the Eleven General Orders of a Sentry while you are in the Delayed Entry Program.”

  1. To take charge of this post and all government property in view.
  2. To walk my post in a military manner, keeping always on the alert and observing everything that takes place within sight or hearing.
  3. To report all violations of orders I am instructed to enforce.
  4. To repeat all calls from posts more distant from the Guardhouse than my own.
  5. To quit my post only when properly relieved.
  6. To receive, obey and pass on to the sentry who relieves me all orders from the Commanding Officer, Command Duty Officer, Officer of the Deck and Officers and Petty Officers of the Watch only.
  7. To talk to no one except in the line of duty.
  8. To give the alarm in case of fire or disorder.
  9. To call the Officer of the Deck in any case not covered by instructions.
  10. To salute all Officers and all colors and standards not cased.
  11. To be especially watchful at night and during the time for challenging, to challenge all persons on or near my post and to allow no one to pass without proper authority.

RTC Maxim

“I will not lie, cheat or steal, nor tolerate those among us who do.”

The guide also directs you to commit additional chapters to memory, including Core Values/Attributes, RTC Chain of Command, Drill & Etiquette, Navy Terminology and Rank & Recognition. Use this course as a starting point and supplement with the full guide.

Module 10

Required Documents & Items for RTC

Checklists

Arriving prepared at RTC starts with having the right documents—and leaving the wrong items at home.

All Recruits Must Have

  • Social Security card.
  • Immunization records.
  • Two forms of valid identification (one must have a photo). The name on all identification must match; if not, bring legal name change documentation.

Acceptable forms of ID include:

  • U.S. Passport or Foreign Passport.
  • Driver's License.
  • U.S. Military Dependent's ID Card.
  • Personal Identity Verification Card.
  • Voter's Registration Card.
  • Certificate of U.S. Citizenship or Certificate of Naturalization.

Do Not Bring to RTC

The guide lists items that must not be brought to Recruit Training. These include, but are not limited to:

  • All alcohol-based health and comfort items.
  • All electronic items.
  • Curling irons.
  • All books and magazines.
  • Cards, dice or other gambling paraphernalia.
  • Large and bulky stationery.
  • Large plastic picks, rakes, or any made of metal.
  • Anything made of glass, including mirrors.
  • Double-edged razor blades.
  • Cigarettes, cigars, pipes, chewing or dipping tobacco.
  • Large deodorants (larger than three ounces).

Always cross-check with the most current official RTC packing list, as policies can change. When in doubt, ask your Recruiter before you ship.
